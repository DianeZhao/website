ACT: Perceptive Acting Under Uncertainty


Can I cross? Is the cyclist seeing me? Will that car actually stop? A familiar train of thoughts for a pedestrian navigating an urban environment: a wrong move could have fatal consequences. Fortunately, humans are masters in predicting each other’s behavior and acting appropriately in ambiguous and uncertain situations, at least when fully alert. This is not always the case: accident statistics show an increase in the number of casualties. These accidents can potentially be overcome by means of automation. While automation offers opportunities to make society safer, it comes with new risks, some of which are fundamental and, others more technological. That is, autonomous agents require an understanding of how humans respond to the uncertainty and risk that they bring. From the agent’s perspective, actions are taken to bring itself into safety if there are uncertainties, but this may introduce new risks for others. For example, a vehicle that comes to a standstill at a strange location, or reduces speed for a green traffic light, has already resulted in accidents, simply because this does not match human expectations. It is a major challenge to develop agents and frameworks that account for uncertainty, risk and interaction in the way humans do. Hence, if we learn from how humans cope with complex and unexpected events, we are not only able to add such abilities to automated agents but can also develop a set of rules for their incorporation in society. This is the true potential that is often suggested but not reached since it cannot be solved from a technological perspective only.

Publications

C1 K. Mustafa, O. de Groot, X. Wang, J. Kober and J. Alonso-Mora, Probabilistic Risk Assessment for Chance-Constrained Collision Avoidance in Uncertain Dynamic Environments, in Proc. IEEE Int. Conf. on Robotics and Automation (ICRA), May 2023.
Links: [PDF],
Abstract: Balancing safety and efficiency when planning in crowded scenarios with uncertain dynamics is challenging where it is imperative to accomplish the robot’s mission without incurring any safety violations. Typically, chance constraints are incorporated into the planning problem to provide probabilistic safety guarantees by imposing an upper bound on the collision probability of the planned trajectory. Yet, this results in overly conservative behavior on the grounds that the gap between the obtained risk and the specified upper limit is not explicitly restricted. To address this issue, we propose a real-time capable approach to quantify the risk associated with planned trajec- tories obtained from multiple probabilistic planners, running in parallel, with different upper bounds of the acceptable risk level. Based on the evaluated risk, the least conservative plan is selected provided that its associated risk is below a specified threshold. In such a way, the proposed approach provides probabilistic safety guarantees by attaining a closer bound to the specified risk, while being applicable to generic uncertainties of moving obstacles. We demonstrate the efficiency of our proposed approach, by improving the performance of a state- of-the-art probabilistic planner, in simulations and experiments using a mobile robot in an environment shared with humans.
